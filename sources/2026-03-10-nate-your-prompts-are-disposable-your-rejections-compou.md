But rejection is where the knowledge gets created. Every time a domain expert looks at AI output, identifies what’s wrong, and explains why, they produce a constraint that didn’t exist before. That constraint is more valuable than the output that triggered it, because the constraint is the only part of the process that compounds. The output is disposable. The rejection is the asset. That means the 17 to 30% where AI gets it wrong is where organizations win or lose. [LINK: guide placeholder] Your most valuable AI skill is saying no Here’s what happens in the moment of rejection. The person applies domain expertise the AI doesn’t have. They identify the specific gap between “looks right” and “is right.” And they articulate a constraint that didn’t exist as an explicit rule before they said it. A strategy partner sends back an AI-generated competitive analysis: “Where’s our proprietary insight on customer switching costs? Any firm with access to the same model could have produced this framing.” She just defined what separates her firm’s work from commodity output. A loan officer rejects a covenant tracking prototype: “You can’t treat a debt service coverage ratio the same as a minimum net worth requirement, they have completely different monitoring triggers.” He just wrote business logic that no requirements doc had captured. An editor kills a draft: “The thesis is buried in paragraph four. Lead with the provocation.” She just encoded an editorial standard.  Every one of those rejections is a knowledge-creation event. That’s the claim, and it’s the thing I’m not hearing anyone say. Not “AI output needs human review”; everyone says that. The specific, non-obvious thing: the moment of rejection is the single most valuable moment in the entire AI workflow. Because the knowledge created in that moment, the constraint, the rule, the encoded taste, is the only part of the process that compounds.  And right now, all of it evaporates. It lives in an email thread, a Slack message, a conversation history. Nobody captures it. Nobody compounds it. The same rejection happens again tomorrow.  I can prove this with the article you’re reading. In the conversation that produced this piece, I told the model not to open with a specific framing. It did it anyway. Twice. The reasoning evaporated between turns. The constraint had to be re-fought and re-won. The tool that was supposed to help produce the work couldn’t even remember the rules for producing it. That’s not an anecdote. That’s the structural problem — happening millions of times a day, in every organization using AI, and nobody has built the infrastructure to fix it. Why this matters now Every AI skill the market rewards in 2026 is a generation skill: prompting, workflow design, tool selection, multi-model orchestration. All aimed at the same thing: producing more, faster. And production has been solved. It’s over. AI can generate a strategy deck, a competitive analysis, a product spec, a working application, a quarter’s worth of content, all before lunch.  The generation side is now effectively a commodity. OpenAI’s GDPval benchmark, the most rigorous measurement yet of AI against actual knowledge work, showed frontier models beating or tying industry professionals with fourteen years of average experience on 70.9% of head-to-head comparisons across forty-four occupations late last year. That number has since climbed to 83%. More than eleven times faster. Less than one percent of the cost. Tasks created by professionals, graded by professionals, and the models are winning most of the comparisons.  Everyone reads this as a story about AI capability. That’s the less interesting reading. The more interesting reading is: if AI now matches your best people’s output 70% of the time on well-specified tasks, what determines whether the other 30% destroys value? And what determines whether the 70% that “looks right” actually is right once it leaves the lab and hits production, where, according to systematic analysis of major agent benchmarks, more than a third of demonstrated capability evaporates?  It’s the same problem in both cases, and it has the same answer. Someone has to be able to look at the output and know. That’s taste. And taste is the most scalable asset in the AI economy, not because it’s easy to develop, but because once you have it, AI gives you a mechanism to encode it, compound it, and deploy it at a scale that was never possible when taste could only express itself through the slow, manual process of doing the work yourself.  The product spec that handles the happy path and misses every edge case that actually matters. The code that demos beautifully and is defenseless in production. The analysis that’s technically correct and wouldn’t change a single decision. The word for this output is slop. And slop doesn’t come from bad AI. It comes from insufficient rejection, from organizations that generate at AI speed and verify at human speed, which means they barely verify at all.  Generation scales with compute. Taste scales with rejection. Compute is a commodity you buy. Taste is an asset you build. The three skills that matter Rejection as a competency has three dimensions, and almost no one is developing any of them deliberately.  Recognition — the ability to detect that something is wrong. This is the part that depends on domain experience and cannot be shortcut. The junior analyst can’t catch a flawed regulatory assumption because she doesn’t know the regulatory landscape yet. The new loan officer can’t spot the covenant logic error because he hasn’t seen enough deals. Recognition is the product of years of practice, and it’s the reason experienced domain experts are becoming more valuable, not less, as AI floods every organization with output. The person who’s reviewed two thousand deals and can feel when something is off is now the most important person in the building, because AI just made everyone else’s output her problem to verify.  And recognition is the dimension most enhanced by AI. A domain expert with strong recognition and access to AI tools can evaluate ten times the output she could before. The leverage is multiplicative. But it only works inside the boundary of her expertise. Inside that boundary, AI is a force multiplier. Outside it, AI is a confidence multiplier. Which is worse.  Articulation — the ability to explain why something is wrong in a way that produces a usable constraint. “This isn’t right” is a rejection. “This isn’t right because you’re treating all covenants identically and DSCR monitoring has completely different triggers than minimum net worth” is a constraint. The difference between those two sentences is the difference between taste that stays in one person’s head and taste that can be encoded, shared, and applied at scale. This is a learnable skill. Almost no organization is teaching it.  GDPval’s methodology inadvertently illustrates why articulation matters. Each task went through five rounds of expert review. Each review round is a rejection event: an expert looked at a task and said “this isn’t representative enough” or “this isn’t clear enough for evaluation.” That iterative refinement through expert rejection is what made the benchmark trustworthy. The expert taste didn’t just evaluate the AI. It built the evaluation infrastructure. Articulation is what turns taste from a personal attribute into an organizational asset.  Encoding — making the constraint persist beyond the moment of rejection. This is where everything breaks down today. The partner articulates the constraint. It lives in an email. Next quarter, a different team makes the same mistake because the constraint was never captured anywhere durable. The reasoning has to be re-created from scratch. The expert’s time, the scarcest resource in the organization, gets burned on the same fight twice.  Andrej Karpathy’s observation that AI systems improve fastest where success can be verified has a direct implication here. The verification infrastructure that enables AI improvement doesn’t emerge from nowhere. It’s built from encoded rejections. Every test suite, every acceptance criterion, every quality gate, every business rule in every system that works well is the residue of someone, somewhere, looking at output and saying no with enough precision that the no could be made permanent. The GCC torture test suite, the standard validation for C compilers, wasn’t designed from first principles. It was built over thirty years by people who encountered failures in the field, articulated the specific constraint being violated, and encoded it as a test. Each entry is a rejection that persisted. The compounding problem (and the compounding opportunity) The economics are worth sitting with. Every rejection that gets properly encoded, captured as a durable, reusable constraint, reduces the expert time needed for future verification. The partner who defined “our work must contain proprietary insight” created a filter that can be applied to every future deliverable, by AI or by humans, without requiring her attention again. The loan officer who specified covenant monitoring logic created a test that can run automatically against every future prototype. Each encoded rejection makes the next round of verification cheaper.  This is a flywheel. Not scaling experts, but scaling the encoded residue of expert judgment. And it compounds across everything the organization touches. A commercial lending software company that serves hundreds of community banks captures different edge cases from each one: agricultural equipment covenants in Texas, SBA documentation in the Midwest, tribal land collateral in the Southwest. Every customer interaction enriches the constraint library. A consulting firm that encodes partner rejections across thousands of engagements builds an institutional definition of quality that no competitor can replicate by subscribing to the same AI APIs. A media company that captures editorial judgment across thousands of pieces develops taste that no individual editor could hold alone.  The companies that have been doing this, even informally, for years already dominate their markets. Epic Systems didn’t win healthcare by having better technology than Cerner or Allscripts. It won by spending forty-five years encoding clinical workflows from thousands of hospitals into a single, deeply integrated platform. When Judy Faulkner sent her entire development team to live in Boston for six months after a failed implementation at Harvard Community Health Plan, they weren’t writing code. They were shadowing doctors, watching workflows, and absorbing the domain constraints that only surface in the gap between what a system produces and what clinicians actually need. The result, decades later: 305 million patient records, near-zero churn, and a system so embedded in clinical operations that switching costs are structural. The moat isn’t the software. It’s the encoded judgment about what the software needs to get right. Built rejection by rejection, workflow by workflow, failure by failure, across thousands of hospitals over decades.  Bloomberg did the same thing in financial data. Every vertical SaaS company that owns its niche did some version of it. What’s new is that AI makes the encoding cycle radically faster: AI generates the provocation, the expert rejects, the rejection gets encoded, the library grows, and the ratio of expert hours to encoded constraints improves with every cycle.  But the infrastructure to make encoding frictionless doesn’t exist yet. This is the gap. The encoding gap Every AI tool is built for generation. Nobody has built the capture layer for institutional taste. The rejection happens in a chat window. The constraint evaporates when the conversation ends. Tomorrow you have the same fight.  This isn’t a small oversight. It’s the largest structural gap in the AI tool ecosystem. Every organization using AI is generating rejections constantly, domain experts looking at output, identifying problems, explaining what’s wrong. Each of those explanations contains a constraint with compounding value. And every single one falls on the floor.  The right solution isn’t a separate tool, not a spreadsheet, not a database, not a dashboard. People won’t context-switch. The capture has to happen where the work already happens: inside the conversation, as a side effect of the rejection you’re already performing. The protocol exists. The classification is straightforward: is this a domain constraint? A quality standard? A business rule? A factual correction? A formatting preference? The storage is a solved problem. The retrieval and surfacing, matching stored constraints to incoming tasks by domain relevance is a well-understood information retrieval problem.  The product that would watch for rejection moments, extract the constraint, persist it, and surface it on relevant future tasks doesn’t exist yet. Someone should build it. It’s the most important unsexy opportunity in AI, because the company that nails constraint capture will do for institutional taste what CRM did for customer relationships: make the intangible tangible, the ephemeral persistent, and the personal organizational.  Think about what that makes possible. Right now, when a domain expert leaves your organization, their taste leaves with them. Twenty years of pattern recognition, quality judgment, and encoded rejection — gone. If their rejections had been captured over those twenty years, the institutional taste persists. The AI system they trained through thousands of corrections keeps performing at their standard. Their judgment compounds beyond their tenure. The organization’s verification frontier, the boundary between domains where it can reliably evaluate AI output and domains where it can’t, stops contracting when people leave and starts expanding as a function of every expert interaction. The seed corn problem There’s one more dimension to this, and it’s the one nobody can reverse.  The LeadDev AI Impact Report found that 54% of engineering leaders believe junior developer hiring will drop in the long term as a direct result of AI coding tools. Google and Meta are hiring roughly 50% fewer new graduates compared to 2021. Salesforce announced it would halt junior hiring entirely.  The pattern is consistent across every dataset. A Harvard study examining 285,000 U.S. firms and 62 million workers from 2015 to 2025 found that when companies adopt generative AI, junior employment drops 8 to 10% within six quarters while senior employment barely changes. Entry-level tech postings collapsed by roughly 67% in the two years following ChatGPT’s release. In the UK, tech graduate roles fell 46% in 2024.  The universal logic: AI handles the production work that juniors used to do, so we need fewer of them.  This is catastrophically wrong. Junior positions are where people develop recognition, the first and most critical dimension of the rejection skill. You learn to spot a flawed deal structure by reviewing hundreds of deals. You learn to catch a weak strategic frame by producing dozens of bad ones and getting them sent back. You learn editorial judgment by writing pieces that get killed and understanding why. That development takes years. It cannot be compressed. And it cannot be replaced by AI, because the skill being developed is precisely the skill of catching what AI gets wrong.  AWS CEO Matt Garman put it directly: “That’s like, one of the dumbest things I’ve ever heard. How’s that going to work when ten years in the future you have no one that has learned anything?” He’s right, and the reason he’s right connects directly to the flywheel. If you eliminate the pipeline that develops recognition, you stop building the articulation capacity that makes rejection precise. If you stop building articulation capacity, you stop generating the encoded constraints that make AI systems better. The constraint library stagnates. The verification frontier stops expanding. And the AI output that depends on that infrastructure for its trustworthiness drifts, imperceptibly, away from correct.  The engineers needed in 2028 to verify agent output won’t exist because they weren’t hired in 2026. The analysts who would have developed the judgment to catch a flawed competitive frame won’t have it because they spent their formative years editing AI output instead of doing original analysis. Today’s juniors are tomorrow’s partners, directors, and principals, the people whose taste defines organizational quality. A 67% hiring collapse in entry-level tech roles means 67% fewer people developing the recognition skills that the entire AI economy depends on.  Remember Karpathy’s framework: what you can verify, you can automate. The corollary should keep every leader up at night. The frontier of AI value is identical to the frontier of your organization’s taste. Where your capacity to verify quality extends, AI creates value. Where it doesn’t, AI creates risk. Not theoretical risk. The compounding, silent risk of an organization that generates more and understands less, every single quarter, and doesn’t know it because the output looks so good. What this means for you this week The anti-slop strategy isn’t “be more careful.” It isn’t “write better prompts.” It’s developing the human skill of rejection — recognition, articulation, encoding — and building the organizational muscle to compound it.  If you’re an executive: the competitive moat is not which AI vendor you choose. Models are commoditized. The moat is the depth and durability of your organization’s encoded taste, the constraint library that makes AI output reliable in your specific domain. Audit it. Where are your domain experts? Are their rejections being captured, or evaporating? Start treating encoded domain judgment as an asset class. Because it is one.  If you manage a team: create space for articulation. When someone rejects AI output, ask them to say why, and make that explanation visible. Not as bureaucracy. As investment. A team that articulates its rejections is building a shared understanding of quality that persists across projects, across personnel changes, across tool migrations. A team that silently fixes AI output and moves on is rebuilding taste from scratch every time.  If you’re an individual contributor: your most valuable professional development isn’t learning the newest AI tool. Tools change quarterly. Domain expertise compounds over years. Deepen your recognition. Practice your articulation, the ability to say exactly why something is wrong, in language precise enough that a system could act on it. The person who’s reviewed two thousand deals and can feel when the numbers are off is the most important person in the building. Not despite AI. Because of it.  If you build AI products: the generation side is a commodity. Every frontier lab ships comparable capability every quarter. The evaluation side, the infrastructure that captures human taste and makes it persistent, compounding, and organizationally durable, is where the next great company gets built. Build the capture layer. Build the encoding infrastructure. Build the product that turns every expert rejection into a constraint that makes the next round of output better without requiring the expert’s attention again. That’s the product the market needs. Nobody’s built it.  That’s the job now. Everything else is a commodity.  I make this Substack thanks to readers like you!
